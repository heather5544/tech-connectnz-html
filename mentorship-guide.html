1. Introduction to TechConnect Mentorship

Welcome to the TechConnect NZ Mentorship Guide. This resource is designed to help both mentors and mentees build successful, rewarding mentorship relationships that foster professional growth and connection within Aotearoa New Zealand's tech community.

About TechConnect NZ

TechConnect NZ connects women and non-binary individuals in the tech industry through mentorship relationships that celebrate diversity and empower career development. Our platform is specifically designed to consider both professional expertise and cultural context when matching mentors and mentees.

The Value of Mentorship

Effective mentorship can be transformative for both parties:

  • For mentees: Guidance, support, industry insights, skill development, networking opportunities, and increased confidence
  • For mentors: Leadership development, fresh perspectives, reinforcement of knowledge, satisfaction from helping others, and community connection

Our Mentorship Philosophy

At TechConnect NZ, we believe effective mentorship is:

  • Reciprocal: Both parties should benefit from the relationship
  • Intentional: Built on clear goals and expectations
  • Inclusive: Respectful of diverse backgrounds, experiences, and perspectives
  • Growth-oriented: Focused on learning and development
  • Bounded: Structured with clear timeframes and commitments
Mentorship Basics

We recommend mentorship relationships include at least 3 sessions, typically meeting monthly. However, the frequency and duration should be agreed upon by both parties based on their needs and availability.

2. Getting Started

Platform Basics

TechConnect NZ makes it easy to connect with potential mentors or mentees. Here's how our platform works:

1
Create Your Profile

Your profile is your introduction to potential mentors or mentees. Be sure to complete it thoroughly, including:

  • Professional background and expertise
  • Goals and interests
  • Optional diversity information (which helps with matching)
  • Preferences for mentorship focus areas

The more complete your profile, the better matches we can suggest.

2
Use the Matching Interface

Our algorithm suggests potential matches based on:

  • Professional compatibility (70%): Skills, experience, goals, and industry
  • Diversity factors (30%): Cultural context, regional connections, and other identity factors you've chosen to share

Navigate to the "Matches" tab to view and explore your suggested connections.

3
Communicate Through the Platform

All initial communications happen through our secure messaging system. This helps both parties:

  • Maintain privacy until they're comfortable sharing contact information
  • Keep a record of conversations
  • Access support if needed

Once a mentorship is established, you can choose to communicate through other channels if preferred.

4
Schedule and Track Meetings

Use our built-in scheduling tool to:

  • Propose meeting times
  • Send calendar invitations
  • Track your mentorship journey
  • Make notes about discussion topics and action items

Finding Your Match

Finding the right mentorship match is key to a successful experience. Here's how to make the most of our matching system:

For Mentees:

  • Be specific about your goals: What skills do you want to develop? What challenges are you facing? What career stage are you in?
  • Consider what you want in a mentor: Experience level, specific expertise, communication style, cultural background
  • Review profiles thoroughly: Look beyond job titles to understand mentors' experiences and approaches
  • Don't rush the process: Take time to consider multiple potential mentors before sending connection requests
Mentee Tip

When reaching out to potential mentors, personalize your message by mentioning specific aspects of their profile that resonated with you and explaining why you believe they would be a good mentor for you.

For Mentors:

  • Clarify what you can offer: Be specific about your areas of expertise, experience, and mentoring style
  • Consider your capacity: Be realistic about how many mentees you can effectively support
  • Look for alignment: Consider whether the mentee's goals match areas where you can provide value
  • Be open to diversity: Consider mentees from different backgrounds who might bring fresh perspectives
Mentor Tip

Before accepting a mentorship request, consider scheduling a brief 15-30 minute initial conversation to gauge compatibility and ensure you can meet the mentee's expectations.

For Mentees Initiating Contact:

  • Be respectful of their time
  • Ask if they'd be open to an initial conversation

3. Structuring the Mentorship

The First Meeting

The initial meeting sets the foundation for your mentorship relationship. It's primarily about getting to know each other and determining if there's a good fit.

Suggested Agenda for First Meeting:

  1. Introductions (15 min): Share your backgrounds, experiences, and what brought you to TechConnect
  2. Discuss expectations (15 min): What does each person hope to gain from the mentorship?
  3. Explore potential focus areas (15 min): What specific topics, skills, or challenges might you work on together?
  4. Discuss logistics (10 min): Meeting frequency, preferred communication methods, potential duration of the mentorship
  5. Next steps (5 min): Decide if you'd like to proceed and schedule your next meeting
First Meeting Format

We recommend holding your first meeting via video call to establish a personal connection. If both parties are in the same region and comfortable with in-person meetings, a coffee shop or other public space can be a good neutral location.

Creating a Mentorship Agreement

A mentorship agreement helps clarify expectations and commitments. While it doesn't need to be formal, documenting your shared understanding can prevent misalignments later.

Key Elements to Include:

  • Goals and focus areas: What you aim to achieve together
  • Meeting frequency and duration: We recommend monthly meetings of 60-90 minutes for at least 3 sessions
  • Communication preferences: How and when to contact each other between meetings
  • Confidentiality: Agreement on what information stays between you
  • Duration: Initial timeframe for the mentorship (can be extended by mutual agreement)
  • Check-in points: When you'll evaluate progress and the relationship
  • Exit strategy: How either party can respectfully end the mentorship if needed

Setting Goals and Expectations

Clear, specific goals help focus your mentorship and make progress measurable. Effective mentorship goals are:

SMART Goal Framework:

  • Specific: Clearly defined outcomes (e.g., "Improve presentation skills," not just "Improve skills")
  • Measurable: How will you know when the goal is achieved?
  • Achievable: Realistic within the timeframe and resources available
  • Relevant: Connected to career development or specific challenges
  • Time-bound: With target dates for milestones and completion

Potential Goal Categories:

Category Sample Goals
Technical Skills - Learn and implement a new technology
- Improve code quality and testing practices
- Develop proficiency in a specific programming language
Career Development - Define a 2-year career advancement plan
- Prepare for a specific promotion
- Transition to a different role within tech
Professional Skills - Improve public speaking and presentation abilities
- Develop leadership skills
- Enhance project management capabilities
Workplace Navigation - Develop strategies for workplace challenges
- Navigate company politics effectively
- Advocate for oneself in meetings and evaluations
Work-Life Balance - Create sustainable boundaries
- Develop stress management techniques
- Navigate industry demands while maintaining wellbeing
Mentee Tip

Limit yourself to 2-3 primary goals for a 3-6 month mentorship. Having too many goals can dilute focus and make it harder to achieve meaningful progress.

Mentor Tip

Help your mentee refine vague goals into specific, actionable objectives. Ask probing questions like "What would success look like?" or "How will we know when you've achieved this?"

4. Conducting Effective Sessions

Preparation

Productive mentorship sessions begin with good preparation from both parties.

For Mentees:

  • Review previous session notes and follow-up on any action items
  • Prepare an agenda with specific topics you want to discuss
  • Identify specific questions for your mentor
  • Reflect on progress toward your mentorship goals
  • Consider challenges you've faced since the last meeting

For Mentors:

  • Review previous session notes and any follow-up items you promised
  • Prepare resources or examples relevant to the mentee's goals
  • Think about questions that might help deepen the discussion
  • Consider your mentee's progress and areas where they might need additional support
  • Reflect on experiences that might be relevant to share

During Meetings

Effective meetings involve active participation from both mentor and mentee. Here are key elements for productive sessions:

Session Structure

A typical 60-minute mentorship session might follow this structure:

  1. Check-in (5-10 min): Brief personal update and setting the session's focus
  2. Progress review (10 min): Discuss progress since the last meeting, including action items
  3. Main discussion (30-35 min): Focus on the primary topics, challenges, or questions
  4. Action planning (10 min): Identify next steps and commitments
  5. Wrap-up (5 min): Summarize key takeaways and confirm the next meeting

Effective Communication Practices

Practice For Mentors For Mentees
Active Listening Focus fully on understanding the mentee's situation before offering advice Listen carefully to advice and perspectives, avoiding defensiveness
Asking Questions Ask open-ended questions that prompt reflection and deeper thinking Ask specific questions that clarify advice or seek concrete examples
Providing Feedback Offer constructive feedback that is specific, actionable, and balanced Be open to feedback and clarify understanding
Sharing Experiences Share relevant experiences without dominating the conversation Connect shared experiences to your own situation
Managing Time Guide the conversation to ensure key topics are covered Respect time boundaries and prioritize discussion topics
Effective Mentorship Questions

For Mentors:

  • "What specific challenge are you facing with [situation]?"
  • "What approaches have you tried so far?"
  • "How do you think that situation could have been handled differently?"
  • "What does success look like for you in this scenario?"
  • "What's one step you could take this week toward your goal?"

For Mentees:

  • "Can you share an example of how you've handled a similar situation?"
  • "What resources would you recommend for learning more about [topic]?"
  • "What skills were most valuable in helping you advance to your current position?"
  • "How would you approach [specific challenge]?"
  • "What feedback do you have on my approach to [situation/task]?"

Follow-up

What happens between sessions is often as important as the sessions themselves. Effective follow-up ensures continuous progress and maximizes the value of your mentorship.

Session Documentation

After each session, it's valuable to document the key points discussed and commitments made. The mentee typically takes the lead on this, but both parties should contribute and agree on the summary.

Between-Session Activities

  • Complete action items agreed upon during the session
  • Reflect on discussions and how insights apply to your work
  • Review relevant resources shared by your mentor/mentee
  • Note questions or topics that arise for the next session
  • Share relevant updates if appropriate (e.g., progress on a project discussed)
Mentee Tip

Keep a mentorship journal to track your progress, insights, and questions between sessions. This helps you recognize your growth and provides material for future discussions.

Mentor Tip

Brief check-ins between formal sessions can be valuable. A quick message asking, "How is your presentation preparation going?" or "Did that approach to your challenge work out?" shows investment in your mentee's progress.

If you encounter persistent issues or uncomfortable situations in your mentorship, don't hesitate to contact TechConnect support at support@techconnect.nz. We're here to help ensure positive experiences for all participants.

Ending a Mentorship

All mentorships eventually come to an end, whether after achieving the original goals, reaching a natural conclusion point, or if the relationship isn't working as expected. Proper closure is important for both parties.

Natural Conclusion

When a mentorship has fulfilled its purpose:

  1. Signal the transition: Discuss approaching the end of the formal mentorship period
  2. Review achievements: Reflect on goals met and progress made
  3. Celebrate successes: Acknowledge growth and accomplishments
  4. Provide mutual feedback: Share what worked well and areas for improvement
  5. Discuss future contact: Decide if and how you'll stay connected

Early Ending

If a mentorship needs to end before achieving its goals:

  • Be honest but respectful: Explain your reasons without blame
  • Provide notice: Give the other person time to adjust (at least one meeting's notice when possible)
  • Acknowledge value: Recognize what was valuable about the experience
  • Offer transition support: Provide resources or suggestions if appropriate
  • Maintain confidentiality: Continue to respect the privacy of your discussions

Many mentorships evolve into more casual professional relationships after the formal period ends. Be clear about expectations for any ongoing contact, such as checking in quarterly or connecting at industry events.

6. Cultural Considerations

TechConnect NZ values the diverse cultural perspectives within Aotearoa New Zealand. Cultural awareness and sensitivity enhance mentorship relationships and create more inclusive experiences.

Te Ao MƒÅori Perspectives

Incorporating Te Ao MƒÅori (the MƒÅori worldview) can enrich mentorship relationships. Consider these concepts:

  • Manaakitanga (Hospitality and Care): Showing respect, generosity, and care in your interactions
  • Whanaungatanga (Relationships and Connections): Recognizing the importance of building relationships and understanding connections
  • Ako (Reciprocal Learning): Acknowledging that both mentor and mentee bring valuable knowledge to share
  • Tuakana-Teina (Relationship Between Senior and Junior): A relationship where the more experienced person (tuakana) helps the less experienced (teina), while recognizing that roles can switch in different contexts
Cultural Introductions

In New Zealand, many professional interactions begin with a brief mihi or introduction that may include sharing your name, where you're from, and your cultural connections. Creating space for this type of introduction can set a positive tone for culturally responsive mentorship.

Cross-Cultural Communication

When mentoring across different cultural backgrounds:

  • Be aware of communication styles: Some cultures value direct communication, while others prefer indirect approaches
  • Ask rather than assume: If unsure about cultural protocols or preferences, respectfully ask
  • Recognize different perspectives: Be open to approaches and solutions influenced by different cultural worldviews
  • Acknowledge the impact of culture: Understand how cultural factors may influence career paths, challenges, and opportunities
  • Create space for cultural expression: Welcome the integration of cultural perspectives in professional development
Mentor Tip

When mentoring someone from a different cultural background, take time to learn about their culture, particularly aspects that may influence professional interactions. This demonstrates respect and helps build trust.

Supporting Diverse Identities

TechConnect NZ focuses on supporting women and non-binary individuals in tech, who may face unique challenges in the industry:

  • Recognize systemic barriers: Understand how gender bias, discrimination, and other structural challenges may affect your mentee's experiences
  • Validate experiences: Listen with empathy when mentees share challenges related to their identity
  • Avoid assumptions: Don't make assumptions about experiences based on gender identity or other aspects of identity
  • Share relevant experiences: If comfortable, share your own experiences navigating identity-related challenges
  • Focus on strengths: Emphasize building on unique perspectives and strengths rather than just overcoming barriers
Mentee Tip

If specific aspects of your identity are important to your professional experience or the support you're seeking, consider sharing this with your mentor. This can help them provide more relevant guidance and support.
